# Deel: The UI/UX Case Study Blueprint
## How to Turn This Project Into a Job-Landing Portfolio Piece

*A guide from two perspectives: what a recruiter scans for, and what a senior designer evaluates in depth.*

---

## THE UNCOMFORTABLE TRUTH BEFORE WE START

> **Recruiter lens**: I spend 8–15 seconds on initial portfolio scan. If your case study doesn't have a clear problem statement, visible process, and measurable outcome in the first scroll, I'm gone. I see 200+ portfolios a week. Yours needs to make me stop scrolling.

> **Senior Designer lens**: I'm looking for *thinking*, not polish. Can you articulate WHY you made a decision? Can you show me a wrong turn and how you recovered? Can you connect a pixel-level choice to a business outcome? If your case study is just pretty mockups with no reasoning, you're a Figma operator, not a designer.

**What makes Deel special as a case study**: It's a real product, in a real market, solving a real problem. You're not redesigning Spotify's playlist page like 10,000 other applicants. You're designing for Kenyan shoppers comparing sugar prices on a 6-inch screen over a 3G connection. That specificity is your advantage.

---

# THE CASE STUDY STRUCTURE (7 Sections)

Here's the complete skeleton. Every section includes what to do, what the recruiter cares about, and what the senior designer evaluates.

---

## SECTION 1: THE HOOK (First 5 Seconds)

### What It Is
The opening of your case study — a hero section that communicates the project at a glance.

### What to Include
- **Project name**: Deel
- **One-line description**: *"A deal aggregator that helps Kenyan shoppers find the best supermarket prices in seconds."*
- **Your role**: Product Designer (sole designer — research, UX, UI, design system)
- **Timeline**: 8 weeks (or however long you spend)
- **A single hero image**: Your final design — the most visually compelling screen. Not a mockup floating in space. Show it on a realistic device, in context.

### 🔴 Recruiter Warning
Do NOT open with "I was tasked with..." or "The client wanted..." — that's passive language. Open with the problem or a provocative stat.

**Bad**: "I designed a deals aggregator for Kenyan supermarkets."
**Good**: *"Kenyan shoppers spend 2+ hours per week comparing prices across 5 different supermarket apps and WhatsApp groups. I designed a single platform that does it in 30 seconds."*

### Senior Designer Note
The hero image should show your highest-fidelity work, but the one-liner should show your *strategic thinking*. The combination signals: "This person can think AND execute."

---

## SECTION 2: PROBLEM DISCOVERY & USER RESEARCH

### What It Is
The evidence that this isn't a made-up project — you actually talked to real people and found real problems.

### What You Need to Do (The Actual Work)

#### A. Define Your Research Questions
Before you talk to anyone, write down what you're trying to learn. NOT "What features do users want?" — that gives you a feature wishlist, not insight.

Better research questions:
1. *"How do Kenyan shoppers currently decide where to buy groceries?"*
2. *"What triggers a shopper to switch supermarkets for a specific trip?"*
3. *"Where does price comparison happen today — mentally, physically, digitally?"*
4. *"What role does WhatsApp play in deal-sharing behavior?"*
5. *"What frustrations do shoppers have with existing supermarket apps/websites?"*

#### B. Recruit & Interview Users (5–8 People)
You need real conversations. Here's who to talk to:

| Segment | Profile | Where to Find Them | Why They Matter |
|---------|---------|--------------------|----|
| **Price-sensitive parent** | 25–40, buys weekly for family of 3–5 | Church groups, estate WhatsApp groups, mama mboga networks | Core user. Highest pain point. |
| **Young professional** | 22–30, lives alone or with roommate, shops at Naivas/Carrefour | Twitter/X Kenya, university alumni groups | Tech-savvy early adopter. Will spread via social. |
| **Bulk shopper / reseller** | Buys in quantity, compares wholesale vs retail | Gikomba area, small kiosk owners | Power user. Would pay for premium features. |
| **WhatsApp deal sharer** | Already forwards supermarket flyers in groups | Any family WhatsApp group | Existing behavior you're formalizing. |

**How to recruit**: Post in 3–4 WhatsApp groups you're already in. *"Naskia bei za grocery zinapanda — nataka kujua venye mtu husort. Anyone willing to chat for 15 min? Nitakununulia kahawa."* Keep it casual. Offer a KES 200 M-Pesa voucher or a coffee.

#### C. Interview Script (30 min max)

```
Opening (2 min):
"I'm exploring how people in Nairobi shop for groceries. 
No right or wrong answers — I just want to understand your routine."

Current behavior (10 min):
- Walk me through your last grocery shopping trip. Where did you go? Why there?
- How did you decide what to buy before you left home?
- Did you compare prices anywhere before going?
- Do you use any supermarket apps? Which ones? What do you like/hate about them?

Deal-finding behavior (10 min):
- Have you ever switched supermarkets to get a better deal? What happened?
- Where do you hear about supermarket offers? (Probe: WhatsApp, flyers, social media, in-store)
- If someone shared a link showing "Sugar is KES 50 cheaper at Quickmart today" — would you change your plans?
- What would make you check a deals app every day?

Pain points (5 min):
- What's the most annoying thing about grocery shopping in Nairobi?
- If you could change one thing about how you find deals, what would it be?

Close (3 min):
"Thank you — this is super helpful. Can I follow up if I have more questions?"
```

#### D. Synthesize: Affinity Mapping
After interviews, extract key quotes and observations. Group them into themes using sticky notes (Miro, FigJam, or physical stickies).

**What the recruiter wants to see**: 
- A photo/screenshot of your affinity map (messy is fine — real > polished)
- 3–5 key insights stated as user needs, NOT feature requests

**Example of a BAD insight**: "Users want a price comparison feature"
**Example of a GOOD insight**: *"Shoppers already compare prices mentally — they carry a rough price map in their heads for 10–15 staple items. They don't need to be taught comparison; they need their existing mental model made faster and more accurate."*

#### E. Create 2–3 User Personas

Don't make generic personas. Make them **Nairobi-specific**:

> **Persona: Wanjiku, 34**
> Lives in South B. Works as an accountant. Shops at Naivas Mombasa Road every Saturday morning. Has 2 kids (ages 4, 7). Buys the same 20 items every week. Checks the Naivas app for offers but finds it slow to load. Forwards flyer screenshots in her "Family Shopping" WhatsApp group. Pain: "I know Quickmart sometimes has cheaper diapers but I don't have time to drive there AND Naivas."

> **Persona: Brian, 26**
> Lives in Kilimani with a roommate. Works in tech. Shops at Carrefour because it's closest. Doesn't track prices — buys whatever's convenient. Would use a price comparison tool if it's on his phone and takes < 30 seconds. Pain: "I feel like I'm overpaying but I'm too lazy to check."

### 🔴 What the Recruiter Scans For in This Section
1. **Did you talk to real people?** (Not "I assumed users would...")
2. **Can you state insights in one sentence?** (Not paragraphs of rambling analysis)
3. **Is the research connected to what you designed?** (Every design decision should trace back to a finding here)

### Senior Designer Evaluation
I'm checking: Did you let the research *change* your assumptions? The best case studies show a moment where the designer learned something unexpected that shifted the direction. If your research perfectly confirmed your assumptions, you either didn't listen or you're fabricating.

## SECTION 3: DEFINING THE PROBLEM (Jobs-to-Be-Done)

### What It Is
Translating raw research into a structured problem definition that drives design decisions.

### What to Produce

#### A. Jobs-to-Be-Done Statements

| Job | Context | Desired Outcome |
|-----|---------|----------------|
| "Help me **find the cheapest price** for items I buy every week" | When planning my Saturday shopping trip | I save KES 200–500 per trip without visiting multiple stores |
| "Help me **discover deals I didn't know about**" | When browsing casually on my phone (e.g., lunch break, matatu) | I feel smart for finding a deal others missed |
| "Help me **decide which store to go to** right now" | When I have 30 minutes and need to pick up 3 things | I go to the one store that's cheapest for *my* specific basket |
| "Help me **share a deal** with someone I care about" | When I see a great price on something my friend/family needs | My friend thanks me and I feel useful |

#### B. The "How Might We" Questions

Convert each JTBD into a design prompt:
- *HMW show shoppers their personalized savings in under 10 seconds?*
- *HMW make discovering new deals feel like a reward, not a chore?*
- *HMW help someone decide between stores without requiring them to know exact prices?*
- *HMW make deal-sharing as natural as forwarding a WhatsApp message?*

#### C. Prioritization Matrix

Plot your HMW questions on an Impact vs. Effort matrix. Show this in the case study. It demonstrates strategic thinking — you didn't try to solve everything at once.

### Senior Designer Evaluation
The JTBD framework shows me you think in terms of *outcomes*, not features. A junior designer says "I'm building a price comparison page." A strong candidate says "I'm helping Wanjiku save KES 300/week without adding any time to her Saturday routine." That reframe is the entire difference.

## SECTION 4: INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE & USER FLOWS

### What It Is
The structural decisions before any visual design.

### What to Produce

#### A. Site Map / App Structure

```
Deel
├── Home (Today's Best Deals)
│   ├── Deal cards (sortable: by %, by amount saved, by category)
│   └── Search bar
├── Compare Prices
│   ├── By Product ("Cooking Oil")
│   └── By Supermarket ("Naivas vs Quickmart")
├── [Supermarket] Deals
│   ├── Naivas
│   ├── Quickmart
│   ├── Carrefour
│   ├── Chandarana
│   └── Game
├── Categories
│   ├── Groceries & Staples
│   ├── Baby Care
│   ├── Household
│   └── ...
├── My Basket (saved deals)
└── For Supermarkets (B2B landing page)
```

#### B. Core User Flows (Draw These in FigJam/Figjam)

**Flow 1: "Saturday Morning Price Check"** (Primary happy path)
```
Open app → See today's top deals → Filter by "my usual items" → 
Compare sugar prices across 3 stores → See "Save KES 85 at Quickmart" → 
Tap to view all Quickmart deals → Share deal to WhatsApp → Close app
(Total time: < 60 seconds)
```

**Flow 2: "Casual Deal Discovery"** (Browse mode)
```
Receive WhatsApp notification "5 new deals under KES 500" → 
Tap link → Land on deals page → Scroll through cards → 
See interesting deal → Tap → View product comparison → 
Save to "My Basket" or Share
```

**Flow 3: "I need diapers — where's cheapest?"** (Task mode)
```
Open app → Search "diapers" → See price comparison table → 
Sorted by price low→high across supermarkets → 
Tap cheapest option → Get directions to nearest branch → Go
```

#### C. Wireframes (Low-Fidelity)

Do these in Figma. Gray boxes, no color, no branding. Show:
- The layout logic
- Content hierarchy (what's biggest? what's first?)
- Interaction patterns (tabs? scroll? filters?)

**Include 2–3 iterations** showing how the wireframe evolved. This is gold for recruiters — it shows process, not just output.

### 🔴 What the Recruiter Scans For
- A clean, readable flow diagram (not a mess of arrows)
- Evidence of iteration (Version A → Version B → Why you changed)
- Mobile-first thinking (your audience is on phones, not desktops)

### Senior Designer Evaluation
I'm looking at your hierarchy decisions. On a deals page, what's the most important information? The price? The discount %? The product name? The supermarket? Your IA choices reveal whether you understand the user's mental model. Hint: for Kenyan shoppers, **the savings amount in KES** matters more than the discount %.  "Save KES 85" means more than "15% off" — because KES 85 is concrete and KES is what they budget in.

## SECTION 5: VISUAL DESIGN & THE DESIGN SYSTEM

### What It Is
The UI layer — where aesthetics, brand identity, and systematic design thinking converge.

### What to Produce

#### A. Moodboard & Design Direction

Before opening Figma, collect 10–15 inspiration screenshots. Sources:
- Other deal/comparison apps: Honey, PriceCheck SA, ShopSavvy, Flipp
- Kenyan digital products: M-Pesa app, Safaricom, Jumia Kenya
- Food/grocery UI patterns: Instacart, Glovo, DoorDash

**Design direction statement** (1 sentence): 
*"Deel should feel fast, trustworthy, and distinctly Kenyan — not a copy of a Western app transplanted into Nairobi. It should feel like your savvy friend who always knows where the best deal is."*

#### B. The Design System (This Is What Gets You Hired)

Build a proper design system in Figma. This is the section senior designers scrutinize most.

**What to include**:

##### Color System

| Token | Value | Usage |
|-------|-------|-------|
| `--color-primary` | (your brand green — something that pops on both light/dark backgrounds) | CTAs, brand elements, "savings" indicators |
| `--color-savings` | A warm green (not the same as primary) | Price savings badges, positive indicators |
| `--color-alert` | Amber/orange | "Expiring soon", urgency markers |
| `--color-surface-primary` | White or very light gray | Card backgrounds |
| `--color-surface-secondary` | Slightly darker | Page backgrounds |
| `--color-text-primary` | Near-black | Body text, prices |
| `--color-text-secondary` | Mid-gray | Labels, metadata |

**Why this matters**: Every color must have a semantic name AND a use case. Don't just pick colors — justify them. *"I chose a warm green over a cool blue for savings indicators because testing showed Kenyan users associated green with M-Pesa — a trusted money context."*

##### Typography Scale

| Level | Size | Weight | Use |
|-------|------|--------|-----|
| Display | 28px | Bold | Deal price callouts |
| Heading 1 | 22px | Semibold | Page titles |
| Heading 2 | 18px | Semibold | Section headers |
| Body | 16px | Regular | Descriptions, list items |
| Caption | 14px | Regular | Metadata, timestamps |
| Badge | 12px | Bold | Discount %, labels |

**Font choice matters**: Use something with good Kenyan locale support. Inter, Plus Jakarta Sans, or DM Sans work well. Avoid overly decorative fonts — this is a utility product. Speed of scanning > aesthetic expression.

##### Component Library (Build These)

**Must-have components (minimum 8–10)**:

1. **Deal Card** — The core UI element. Shows: product image, product name, current price, old price (struck through), savings amount, supermarket badge, category tag. Has states: default, hovered, pressed, saved.

2. **Price Comparison Row** — Shows one product across multiple supermarkets. Highlights the cheapest. Has a "savings vs. most expensive" indicator.

3. **Supermarket Badge** — Small pill showing the supermarket name + brand color. Naivas = green, Carrefour = blue, Quickmart = red.

4. **Filter Bar** — Category pills (scrollable horizontal), sort dropdown (Price ↑, Savings ↓, Latest).

5. **Search Bar** — With recent searches, suggested searches ("Popular: cooking oil, diapers, sugar").

6. **Savings Summary Banner** — "You could save KES 847 this week." Sticky banner that updates based on viewed deals.

7. **Share Button** — WhatsApp-specific share CTA (WhatsApp green, WhatsApp icon). Not a generic share sheet.

8. **Empty State** — What users see when search returns nothing, or when a category is empty.

9. **Skeleton Loader** — Loading state that mimics the deal card shape (important for 3G/4G connections).

10. **Notification Card** — WhatsApp-style message preview for deal alerts.

##### Spacing & Grid

```
Base unit: 4px

Spacing scale:
xs:  4px   (tight padding inside badges)
sm:  8px   (between badge and text)
md:  16px  (card padding, element gaps)
lg:  24px  (section spacing)
xl:  32px  (page-level spacing)

Grid: 
Mobile: 4px base, 16px margin, 8px gutter, flexible columns
Tablet: 4px base, 24px margin, 16px gutter, 12-column
```

#### C. High-Fidelity Screens (8–12 Screens)

Design these in Figma:

| Screen | Priority | What It Proves |
|--------|----------|---------------|
| **Home — Today's Deals** | 🔴 Must | Your core UI. Visual hierarchy, card design, scannability. |
| **Product Price Comparison** | 🔴 Must | Table/card layout for cross-store comparison. Information design skill. |
| **Search Results** | 🔴 Must | How you handle variable content, filters, and zero-results state. |
| **Supermarket Deals Page** | 🟡 Should | Filter and sort patterns on category-specific content. |
| **Deal Detail / Product Page** | 🟡 Should | Expanded view with price history chart. Data visualization skill. |
| **Share to WhatsApp Flow** | 🟡 Should | Interaction design. Shows you think about sharing as a growth mechanic. |
| **Onboarding (2–3 screens)** | 🟢 Nice | First-time user experience. Permission request patterns. |
| **Empty/Error States** | 🟢 Nice | Shows design maturity — juniors forget these exist. |
| **"For Supermarkets" Landing** | 🟢 Nice | B2B design skill. Completely different audience, tone, and goals. |

#### D. Interaction & Motion Design

If you can, add Figma prototyping or use Principle/ProtoPie for:
- Deal card tap → expand animation
- Pull-to-refresh on deals list
- Filter pill selection → content reorganization
- Share flow → WhatsApp deep link

**Even simple Smart Animate transitions in Figma show motion awareness.** This separates mid-level from senior candidates.

### 🔴 What the Recruiter Scans For
1. **Is the design system documented, not just scattered frames?** → Use a dedicated Figma page labeled "Design System"
2. **Are components using Auto Layout and variants?** → This proves production readiness
3. **Does the visual design feel cohesive?** → Every screen should unmistakably belong to the same product
4. **Is it mobile-first?** → If your hero mockup is a desktop screen for a Kenyan grocery app, it's a red flag

### Senior Designer Evaluation
I'm checking three things:
1. **Naming conventions** in Figma — are your layers named `Frame 247` or `DealCard/Default/Naivas`? The latter tells me you've worked on real teams.
2. **Edge cases** — did you design the state where a product has no image? Where the price hasn't changed? Where the product name is 60 characters long? Edge cases separate portfolios from production-ready design.
3. **Accessibility** — contrast ratios, tap target sizes (minimum 44×44px), font sizes readable at arm's length. If your body text is 12px on a mobile screen, you don't understand your user's context (they might be looking at this in a matatu with sunlight glare).

## SECTION 6: USABILITY TESTING & ITERATION

### What It Is
Proof that you tested your designs with real humans and changed things based on what you learned.

### What to Do

#### A. Recruit 3–5 Testers
Same profiles as your research participants. Can be different people or the same ones (follow-up is fine).

#### B. Create a Figma Prototype
Link your high-fidelity screens into a clickable prototype. Cover 2–3 task flows:
- **Task 1**: "Find the cheapest cooking oil in Nairobi right now."
- **Task 2**: "Your friend asked about diaper prices. Share the cheapest option on WhatsApp."
- **Task 3**: "You're going to Naivas this Saturday. What deals should you look for?"

#### C. Run the Test (20 min per person)

```
Script:
"I'm going to show you a prototype of an app. It's not fully built — 
some things won't work. I want to see how you'd use it naturally. 
Think out loud — tell me what you're thinking as you go."

Then give them each task. Time them. Note where they:
- Pause (confusion)
- Tap the wrong thing (misaligned mental model)
- Express delight ("Oh that's nice! I can see exactly how much I'd save")
- Express frustration ("Where do I go back?")
```

#### D. Document Findings as a Table

| Finding | Severity | Evidence | Change Made |
|---------|----------|----------|-------------|
| 3/5 users didn't notice the filter bar | 🔴 High | Users scrolled past it; 2 said "How do I find just food?" | Moved filters above the first card. Made them sticky on scroll. |
| Users loved the "Save KES X" badge | 🟢 Positive | 4/5 said this was the first thing they looked at | Increased badge size by 20%. Made it the visual anchor. |
| "Share to WhatsApp" icon was mistaken for a phone call button | 🟡 Medium | 2/5 hesitated before tapping | Changed to WhatsApp brand icon + added "Share" label |
| Users expected to tap the supermarket logo to see all deals from that store | 🟡 Medium | 3/5 tapped the logo, which wasn't interactive | Made supermarket badges clickable → navigates to store deals page |

**This table is the most powerful thing in your entire case study.** It proves you can take feedback, not just give it.

#### E. Show Before/After

For your top 2–3 changes, show side-by-side: the original design → the iterated design, with a one-line explanation of WHY.

### 🔴 What the Recruiter Scans For
- **Did you actually test?** Photos of testing sessions or screenshots of the video screen are proof.
- **Did you change anything?** If your "final" design is identical to your "first" design, you either didn't test or didn't listen.
- **Is the table clean?** Findings → Severity → Change Made. This is the format design leads use. Using it shows you're ready for a team.

### Senior Designer Evaluation
The quality of your findings tells me your observation skill. "The user didn't like it" is a bad finding. "The user's thumb hovered over the filter bar for 3 seconds, then bypassed it — suggesting the visual weight was insufficient to communicate interactivity" is a great finding. **Describe behavior, not feelings.**

## SECTION 7: BUSINESS METRICS & IMPACT

### What It Is
Connecting your design work to business outcomes. This is what separates a portfolio piece from a product design case study.

### What to Include

#### A. Define the Metrics Your Design Affects

| Metric | Definition | How Your Design Influences It | Target |
|--------|-----------|-------------------------------|--------|
| **Time to first deal found** | Seconds from app open to first deal interaction | Homepage layout, search UX, deal card scannability | < 10 seconds |
| **Deals viewed per session** | Avg number of deal cards expanded/clicked | Card density, scroll behavior, filter discoverability | 5+ per session |
| **Share rate** | % of sessions where a deal is shared via WhatsApp | Share button prominence, pre-formatted share message, social proof | 15%+ |
| **Return rate (Day 7)** | % of users who return within 7 days | Personalization, freshness signals, notification design | 30%+ |
| **Click-through to supermarket** | % of deal views that result in a tap to the supermarket's site | CTA design, trust signals, deal detail page layout | 20%+ |
| **Search success rate** | % of searches that result in a deal interaction | Search results quality, empty state recovery, category suggestion UX | 70%+ |

#### B. The "Design Impact" Narrative

Write 2–3 paragraphs connecting specific design decisions to metric predictions:

*"By placing the savings amount (in KES) as the primary visual element on each deal card — larger than the product name and price — I prioritized the user's core motivation: knowing exactly how much they'll save. Based on usability testing, 4 of 5 participants said the savings badge was the first thing they noticed, suggesting high scannability. This design choice directly supports the 'Time to first deal found' metric by reducing cognitive load at the card level."*

#### C. Proposed A/B Tests (Shows Growth Mindset)

| Test | Hypothesis | Variants |
|------|-----------|----------|
| Savings display format | Showing "Save KES 85" outperforms "15% off" for tap-through rate | A: "Save KES 85" / B: "15% off" / C: Both |
| WhatsApp share placement | A floating WhatsApp FAB outperforms an inline share icon | A: Inline icon / B: Floating FAB |
| Deal card image size | Larger images increase engagement but slower load on 3G | A: 80×80px / B: 120×120px thumb / C: No image |

#### D. Connect Design to the Business Thesis

This is where you bring it all together. One paragraph:

*"Deel's growth depends on two flywheels: shopper engagement (which creates data and traffic) and supermarket participation (which creates deal supply). Every design decision in this case study was evaluated against both flywheels. The WhatsApp share mechanic drives organic shopper growth (flywheel 1). The 'Savings Summary Banner' creates proof of platform value that can be shown to supermarket partners (flywheel 2). The design system's supermarket badge component was deliberately built to scale — adding a 6th supermarket is a color change and logo swap, not a redesign."*

### 🔴 What the Recruiter Scans For
- **Can this person talk about business impact?** Most junior designers can't. If you can, you jump ahead of 80% of applicants.
- **Are the metrics relevant?** If you're tracking "number of Figma frames" as a metric, you don't understand product.

### Senior Designer Evaluation
I'm looking for honesty. You don't have real analytics yet (the product isn't fully launched). That's fine. What I want to see is: can you *predict* what to measure, *propose* how to test, and *reason* about trade-offs? That's the thinking that makes someone promotable.

# THE PORTFOLIO PRESENTATION FORMAT

## Option A: Behance / Personal Website (Recommended)

Structure: 
1. Hero section (project name, one-liner, hero image)
2. Problem & Research (keep tight — recruiters skim)
3. Key Insights (3–5 bullet points with user quotes)
4. Design Process (IA → Wireframes → UI → Iteration, show progression)
5. Design System (dedicated section — screenshot of your Figma component page)
6. Final Designs (high-fidelity mockups — use realistic device frames)
7. Metrics & Impact (business connection)
8. Reflections (what you'd do differently — shows maturity)

**Total reading time: 5–7 minutes.** Any longer and recruiters bounce.

## Option B: Figma Case Study File

Create a Figma file specifically for the case study presentation:
- Page 1: Cover
- Page 2: Problem & Research
- Page 3: User Flows & IA
- Page 4: Wireframes to High-Fi progression
- Page 5: Design System
- Page 6: Final screens (annotated)
- Page 7: Usability Testing Results
- Page 8: Metrics

Share this as a view-only Figma link. Some recruiters prefer this because they can see your Figma skills directly.

# THE RECRUITER'S SCORECARD (What I'm Actually Grading)

| Criteria | Weight | What "Great" Looks Like |
|----------|--------|------------------------|
| **Problem clarity** | 15% | I understand the problem in < 10 seconds |
| **Research quality** | 15% | Real users, real quotes, unexpected insights |
| **Design process** | 20% | Visible progression from messy → refined, with rationale for changes |
| **Visual execution** | 20% | Professional, consistent, system-driven design that feels intentional |
| **Systems thinking** | 15% | Design system documented, components are reusable and named properly |
| **Business awareness** | 10% | Can connect design choices to user outcomes and business metrics |
| **Communication** | 5% | Clean writing, logical flow, no typos, appropriate length |

### What Gets You an Interview

- A case study that hits 70%+ across all criteria
- At least ONE "wow" moment (a unique insight, a beautiful interaction, a clever solution to a hard constraint)
- Evidence you can work within real-world constraints (budget, tech, market)
- Kenyan market context shows you can design for *your* users, not generic "users"

### What Gets You Rejected

- All mockup, no process ("I opened Figma and designed this")
- No user research (or obviously fabricated research)
- Generic design that could be any deals app in any country
- 20+ minute case study that doesn't respect the reader's time
- Redesigns of existing products with no original thinking

# THE WORK PLAN (What to Do and When)

| Phase | Duration | Deliverable |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **User Research** | Week 1–2 | 5+ interview recordings/notes, affinity map, 2–3 personas, key insights document |
| **Problem Definition** | Week 2 | JTBD statements, HMW questions, prioritization matrix |
| **IA & Flows** | Week 2–3 | Sitemap, 3 user flows, low-fi wireframes (3 rounds of iteration) |
| **Design System** | Week 3–4 | Color, type, spacing, 10+ components in Figma with variants and Auto Layout |
| **High-Fidelity Design** | Week 4–5 | 8–12 screens, full prototype linked in Figma |
| **Usability Testing** | Week 5–6 | 3–5 test sessions, findings table, before/after iterations |
| **Metrics & Impact** | Week 6 | Metrics table, A/B test proposals, business narrative |
| **Case Study Write-Up** | Week 6–7 | Behance/portfolio page or Figma presentation file |
| **Polish & Review** | Week 7–8 | Peer review (ask 2 designers for feedback), final edits |

# FINAL ADVICE

### From the Recruiter
*"I don't hire designers who can make things pretty. I hire designers who can make things work for real people in real contexts. Deel is a Kenyan product for Kenyan shoppers — if your case study feels like it could be transplanted to San Francisco without any changes, you've missed the point. Make it specific. Make it local. Make it real."*

### From the Senior Designer
